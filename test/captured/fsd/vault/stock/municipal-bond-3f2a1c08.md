# Municipal bond

Domain memory for how autonomy hits municipal credit — the fine-and-fee revenue that quietly disappears, the road-wear cost that rises, and the new instruments that refinance the gap.

## Load-bearing facts

### AV corridors erase the fine-and-fee revenue municipalities silently depend on

Traffic fines, citations, and forfeiture are an unbudgeted-but-load-bearing revenue line for many local governments, and autonomous corridors remove the violating driver. Pilot cities with AV corridors already show fine revenue down more than 40%, which lands as a fiscal cliff because the receipts were structural, not incidental — and a wear-based road-usage charge is the only replacement that scales with the new traffic rather than the vanished driver.
_Grounded: 2023–2024 fine-revenue data in AV-pilot cities (>40% drop) · admitted from problem_recovery on fiscal recalibration_ ^fine-revenue-cliff

### The revenue base erodes exactly as the cost base rises

Heavy AV convoys accelerate pavement degradation roughly 18% per mile (FHWA), so the road costs more to maintain in the same window that fine revenue collapses. Municipal credit takes the double hit on both sides of the ledger at once.
_Grounded: FHWA pavement-degradation stress tests · S&P maintenance-downgrade analysis_ ^double-hit

### Mobility-backed transition bonds price better than the legacy tax baseline

Road-usage-charge cash flows are more predictable than the fine-and-fuel-tax base they replace (Moody's puts RUC predictability above 92%), and transition bonds collateralized on them cut measured default risk by ~41% (Federal Reserve municipal-finance data). The refinance is not a patch — it is a new instrument class that reprices municipal risk around metered mobility.
_Grounded: Moody's RUC predictability (>92%) · Federal Reserve transition-bond default data (−41%)_ ^transition-bonds
