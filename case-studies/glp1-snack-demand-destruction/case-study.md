# Case Study: GLP-1s In The Grocery Basket

In 2026, GLP-1 drugs are no longer only a diabetes-care story or a celebrity
weight-loss story. A KFF poll from late 2025 found that 12% of U.S. adults were
currently taking a GLP-1 drug such as Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, or Zepbound,
and 18% had taken one at some point.

The grocery aisle is starting to show it. A household panel study published in
2026 found that households with a GLP-1 user cut grocery spending by about 5%
within six months of adoption. The reductions were larger in higher-income
households and heavier in calorie-dense processed categories. Savory snacks,
sweet baked goods, candy, sugary drinks, and limited-service restaurant visits
all look more exposed than basic staples. A few categories, such as yogurt and
some protein-forward foods, moved the other way.

For a packaged-food company, this does not arrive as one clean signal. The same
weekly report can show fewer impulse units, stronger interest in protein,
smaller portion packs, private-label pressure, normal inflation noise, and a
retailer asking for a new "GLP-1 friendly" shelf story. The consumer may be the
person taking the drug, but the basket belongs to a household. One person eating
less at night can change what gets bought for everyone else. If the person stops
taking the drug, some spending may drift back.

The public conversation is still mostly about weight loss, side effects, drug
pricing, and whether food companies can launch healthier snacks. Inside a
category team, the harder question is different. A salty-snack line, a candy
aisle, a frozen-meal brand, and a grocer's front-end impulse rack do not have the
same exposure, margin structure, or replacement occasions.

The team has to decide what it is seeing while the market is still moving. Is
this a temporary diet wave, a product-mix shift, a household-level demand change,
or something else? Which lost occasions can be replaced, which only shrink, and
which disappear?

## Synopsis

With roughly 12% of US adults on a GLP-1 drug, household grocery spending (especially impulse categories) is starting to show measurable decline.
