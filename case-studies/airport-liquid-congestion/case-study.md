# Case Study: Airport Liquid Rule Congestion

The airport security checkpoint has a recurring slowdown. Passengers arrive at
the X-ray belt with drink bottles, cosmetics, gels, or other containers that do
not comply with the carry-on liquid rule. By the time the problem is found, the
bag is already in the screening lane.

The interruption is small in isolation and ugly in aggregate. Staff stop the
flow, pull the bag aside, explain the rule, wait while the passenger searches,
argue about edge cases, discard items, or send the passenger away to repack. The
lane loses rhythm, the queue watches, and the passenger feels punished for a
mistake discovered too late.

The airport has already tried the obvious public-information layer: signs,
website copy, airline reminders, and announcements. They help some passengers
but not enough. The failures are concentrated among rushed travelers, families,
infrequent flyers, passengers using cabin bags only, and people who saw the rule
but did not translate it into an action before reaching the belt.

What could the airport change so fewer liquid problems reach the screening lane,
without weakening inspection, adding a worse queue, or turning the rule into a
long legal explanation?
