# Case Study: The Switch Under The Fleet

As of June 22, 2026, treat this as a Tesla case. The premise is not that every
road, weather condition, regulator, or consumer has already yielded. The premise is
that Tesla-scale full self-driving works well enough that the binding question has
moved from invention to permission, rollout, and adoption.

The current signals are concrete. Tesla's Robotaxi service is operating in limited
areas of Austin, Dallas, and Houston, Texas, using Model Y vehicles and a dedicated
Robotaxi app. Texas now requires commercial automated-vehicle operators to hold a
state authorization for vehicles operating without a human driver. California has
Tesla Robotaxi LLC on its testing-with-driver permit list, but not on its driverless
testing or deployment lists. California's new autonomous-vehicle rules also create a
staged path through safety-driver testing, driverless testing, and deployment, with
traffic-law accountability aimed at the operator rather than a missing human driver.

Tesla also says Full Self-Driving (Supervised) has rolled out beyond North America:
Australia, New Zealand, South Korea, the Netherlands, Lithuania, Estonia, Denmark,
and Belgium are listed alongside the United States, Canada, Mexico, and Puerto Rico.
That supervised product still requires an attentive driver. It does not make a
consumer Tesla legally autonomous. But it matters because the same company is pushing
two related gates at once: supervised capability spreading through owned cars, and
commercial driverless service beginning in limited Texas markets.

That makes the discontinuity different from a small robotaxi fleet. The hard
question is what happens if autonomy can be distributed through an installed vehicle
base, a shared software stack, and over-the-air updates rather than through slow,
city-by-city fleet construction.

The public story is still shallow. Most people see cheaper rides, displaced Uber and
taxi drivers, and a fight over whether the cars should be allowed on certain streets.
That is not wrong. It is just the smallest visible surface.

Driving is not one industry. It is a load-bearing behavior under insurance, repair,
freight, public revenue, identity, land use, airport access, policing, commute
geography, household balance sheets, and local labor markets. Some parts depend on
people driving badly. Some depend on people owning cars. Some depend on people being
stuck behind the wheel. Some depend on the state having millions of individual
drivers to license, stop, ticket, insure, and tax.

If the Tesla premise holds, the first wave will look mundane: a few service areas,
permit fights, pricing experiments, cautious expansion, bad headlines after
individual incidents, and arguments about safety statistics. The deeper question is
which systems are quietly built on the assumption that human driving remains normal.

## Decision Point

You are advising someone who accepts the Tesla premise before most institutions
around the road economy have updated.

Which dependent systems deserve separate investigation first, and how would you tell
which ones merely get cheaper, which ones lose the event they were built around,
and which ones move from individual drivers to fleet-scale operators?

## Synopsis

Granting that Tesla-scale full self-driving works well enough, the binding question shifts from invention to permission, rollout, and adoption.
