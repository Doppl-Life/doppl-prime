# Case Study: Heinz Ketchup Bottle Authenticity

Heinz has a table-service problem in some restaurants and street-food venues.
The customer sees the familiar Heinz bottle, but the brand no longer controls
what is inside it. A venue can buy Heinz bottles, refill them with cheaper
ketchup, and keep presenting the bottle as if nothing changed.

The deception is awkward to police. Contracts and distributor pressure may deter
some venues, but they do not help the customer at the table. Taste is too slow
and too subjective. A phone-based check would add friction to a condiment. A
heavy tamper-proof package would be expensive and wasteful for a bottle that
often lives in ordinary restaurant service.

Heinz needs a cheap, familiar package-level change that makes substitution
harder to hide during normal use.

What could the bottle do, without asking the customer to scan, read, or perform
an authentication ritual?

## Synopsis

Venues refill branded Heinz bottles with cheaper ketchup, and the brand cannot control or prove what is actually inside the bottle at the table.
