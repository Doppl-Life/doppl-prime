# Case Study: The Car You Stop Owning

Americans do not just use cars. They finance them, insure them, trade them in,
repair them, park them, register them, borrow against them, build garages for them,
and organize daily life around having one nearby.

Tesla-scale autonomy puts pressure on that ownership pattern. Robotaxi service is
already live in limited Texas areas. Supervised FSD is spreading through owned Tesla
vehicles in multiple countries, while still requiring an attentive driver. The
combined signal is not just that rides may get cheaper. It is that one company is
trying to make autonomy part of a vehicle platform that can be sold, subscribed to,
updated, and possibly used as a service asset.

The U.S. ownership stack is huge. Auto-loan balances were about $1.69 trillion in
Q1 2026. Franchised light-vehicle dealers sold 16.2 million light-duty vehicles in
2025, generated more than $1.3 trillion in sales, and wrote more than 276 million
repair orders. Dealerships are not only showrooms; they are finance desks, trade-in
funnels, local employers, service businesses, warranty channels, political actors,
and beneficiaries of state franchise laws.

The private car also has a utilization problem. It is expensive and sits unused for
most of the day. That made sense when the alternative was waiting, walking, taking
transit that did not match the trip, or paying another human to drive. If a reliable
autonomous ride is always near, the household car has to justify itself differently.

There are at least three live possibilities:

- households keep owning cars because autonomy makes the owned car more useful
- households own fewer cars because cheap service covers ordinary trips
- ownership mutates because a household vehicle can work as a paid robotaxi when
  the owner is not using it

Those paths hit different institutions. A pure service model pressures retail unit
sales, used-car residuals, auto lending, parking, fuel retail, and household
insurance. A household robotaxi model may preserve financing but change what is
being financed: not a depreciating convenience asset, but an income-producing
machine with different risk, wear, tax, repair, and liability questions.

The visible story is again too small. People can understand cheaper rides and driver
job loss. Far fewer are asking what happens to the car as the default household
asset.

## Decision Point

You are looking at the ownership economy before the market settles.

Which parts depend on households buying and maintaining privately used vehicles, and
which parts can survive if mobility shifts toward fleets, subscriptions, or
income-producing owned cars?

## Synopsis

American daily life is organized around owning a car (financing, insurance, repair, parking); the case examines how autonomy pressures that whole ownership pattern.
