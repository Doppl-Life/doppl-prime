# Case Study: London Underground Passenger Map

By the early 1930s, London's Underground had become too complex for the old style
of passenger map. The lines spread far outside the central city, stations were
unevenly spaced, and the dense central network was exactly where riders needed
the most clarity.

A geographically faithful map had an obvious virtue: it showed where things
really were. But it also created practical problems. Central stations crowded
together. Outer branches consumed space. The shape of the city competed with the
shape of the rail system. Riders trying to answer ordinary journey questions
could be forced to parse street geography they did not need.

Most passengers were not using the Underground map to walk between stations or
measure exact surface distance. They needed to know which line to take, where to
change, and how stops connected.

What kind of map would make the railway easier to use, even if it made a
geographer uncomfortable?
