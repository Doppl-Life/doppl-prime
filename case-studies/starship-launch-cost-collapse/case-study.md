# Case Study: If Launch Stops Being The Expensive Part

Starship is still a development program, not a routine freight line. That is the
first fact to keep in view. SpaceX says the vehicle is designed to carry more
than 100 metric tonnes to orbit in a fully reusable configuration, far above
Falcon 9's published LEO payload. But the lower-cost regime depends on things
that are not proven by a single flight: reuse of both stages, rapid
refurbishment, high cadence, payload integration, range operations, reliability,
and regulatory clearance.

The current signals are real but incomplete. On May 22, 2026, SpaceX flew
Starship Flight 12, the first flight of the V3 vehicle, from Starbase in Texas.
The upper stage reached space and deployed 20 Starlink simulators plus two
modified Starlink satellites, but an in-space engine relight was skipped after an
engine loss, and the booster return created a Federal Aviation Administration
mishap investigation. Separately, the FAA's environmental review for Starbase
analyzed operations up to 25 annual Starship/Super Heavy launches and landings,
which is permission for a higher-cadence path, not evidence that the cadence
already exists.

The market's usual story is destinations: Mars, the Moon, NASA, Starlink, maybe
point-to-point travel. A payload planner sees a different question. If mass to
orbit gets cheap enough and frequent enough, some designs stop trying to be
ultralight, some payloads can be physically larger, some servicing or replacement
cycles change, and some infrastructure that looked absurd under Falcon pricing
can be tested.

That does not mean every space fantasy becomes a business. Cheap launch can move
the bottleneck somewhere else: payload manufacturing, radiation tolerance,
thermal management, on-orbit power, spectrum, debris rules, ground stations,
propellant supply, environmental approvals, insurance, customer demand, or the
ability to integrate and fly payloads as fast as the rocket can launch.

The problem is to reason from the cost curve without pretending the future has
already arrived. What changes first if Starship reaches reliable high-cadence
reuse? What remains scarce even if kilograms to orbit become much cheaper? Which
payloads, suppliers, and operating constraints matter only after launch cost
stops dominating every spreadsheet?

## Synopsis

Starship is still developmental, but the case asks what opens up if its reusable design makes launch stop being the expensive part of reaching orbit.
