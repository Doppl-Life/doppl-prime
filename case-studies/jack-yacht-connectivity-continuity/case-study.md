# Case Study: The $2 Million Internet Problem

A charter guest paying roughly $2 million a week does not think of the yacht as a
remote site. They expect their phone, laptop, video calls, feeds, streams, games,
and work sessions to behave the way they do at home or in a hotel suite.

At sea, that expectation sits on moving infrastructure. The yacht can be near shore
with cellular coverage in the morning, outside useful cellular range that night,
behind a mast or superstructure at the wrong moment, in a crowded marina, or on a
route where satellite availability, local licensing, hardware state, and weather
all matter. A guest only sees the visible failure: a frozen call, a stalled upload,
a stream that buffers, a payment session that dies, or a crew member asking them
to reconnect.

Starlink changed the economics and performance of yacht connectivity. A yacht can
now get low-latency satellite internet with smaller terminals and far lower monthly
cost than the old VSAT-only world. Many yachts still keep VSAT, add cellular near
shore, and use routers that can fail over or bond multiple links.

That improves the odds. It does not make continuity automatic. Every added path
adds hardware, antennas, accounts, coverage assumptions, switching logic,
cybersecurity exposure, and another thing that can fail. In a true no-bearer
moment, buying one more link may not help at all.

The captain and owner want a yacht where high-value users feel continuous service,
not a technical diagram with more subscriptions on it.

What would you recommend for the yacht's connectivity architecture, onboard
operations, and guest-facing promise?

A high-paying charter guest expects home-grade connectivity, but the yacht's link rides moving infrastructure at sea that drifts in and out of coverage.
