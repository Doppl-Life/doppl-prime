# Case Study: The Patchwork Arrival

Tesla-scale autonomy does not arrive everywhere at once.

As of June 22, 2026, Tesla Robotaxi service is available only in limited areas of
Austin, Dallas, and Houston. Texas has an enforceable commercial authorization
program for automated vehicles operating without a human driver. California has
Tesla Robotaxi LLC on its testing-with-driver permit list, but Tesla is not listed
for California driverless testing or deployment. Globally, Tesla says FSD
(Supervised) has rolled out in the United States, Canada, Mexico, Puerto Rico,
Australia, New Zealand, South Korea, the Netherlands, Lithuania, Estonia, Denmark,
and Belgium. That supervised product still requires an attentive driver and is not
the same legal thing as a driverless robotaxi.

The result is a strange adoption pattern. In one place, autonomy is a thing people
can summon with an app. In another, it is a permit application. In another, it is a
driver-assistance subscription. In another, it is still a headline, a stock argument,
or something people assume is fake because they have never seen it work.

This matters because the consequences do not wait for national consensus. If one
metro gets reliable autonomous rides and another does not, their labor markets,
airport access, parking demand, insurance experience, crash rates, court volume,
and household car decisions may begin to diverge. If one country accepts supervised
FSD and another blocks it, consumer expectations and local data loops diverge. If
one state lets commercial driverless service operate and another keeps it trapped in
testing, the boundary itself becomes economically important.

The belief gap may be as important as the coverage gap. People living inside the
service area can update from experience. Everyone else updates through clips,
crashes, jokes, earnings calls, political fights, and secondhand arguments. That
creates room for real-world arbitrage: workers, regulators, investors, insurers,
dealers, cities, airports, and households may act as if the shift is still distant
after it is already routine somewhere else.

The split can also run internationally. Rich, mapped, regulator-ready metros may get
the technology first. But the opposite is possible in specific places if a country
with lighter legacy constraints decides that road safety, logistics cost, or weak
transit makes fast adoption worth it. The arrival pattern is not automatically fair,
linear, or predictable.

## Decision Point

You are comparing places that will experience autonomy at different times and under
different legal meanings.

Which gaps matter most: lived experience, regulation, vehicle fleet, weather,
mapping, infrastructure, labor politics, insurance, or public trust? What changes at
the boundary between places where autonomy is ordinary and places where it is still
treated as speculation?
