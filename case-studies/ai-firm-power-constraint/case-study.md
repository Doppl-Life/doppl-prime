# Case Study: The AI Campus That Cannot Just Plug In

In 2026, a frontier AI buildout is no longer only a chip-procurement problem. A
large training or inference campus needs accelerators, but it also needs a site
that can take enormous electrical load, cool dense racks, get permits, connect to
the grid, and operate on a schedule that does not match normal utility planning.

The numbers have moved fast. The International Energy Agency estimated data
centres used about 415 TWh of electricity in 2024, roughly 1.5% of global
electricity consumption, and projected that global data-centre electricity use
could reach about 945 TWh by 2030. Accelerated servers, driven mainly by AI, are
projected to grow much faster than conventional server demand.

The grid does not move like a GPU supply chain. Berkeley Lab's interconnection
queue data showed more than 2,060 GW of generation and storage capacity seeking
connection to the U.S. grid at the end of 2025, with many projects withdrawn and
completed projects taking longer to study and energize. A site can have capital,
land, and ordered hardware while still waiting on transmission upgrades,
substations, local opposition, water constraints, or a utility service agreement.

The behavior of large buyers has changed. Microsoft signed a 20-year agreement
with Constellation to support restarting Three Mile Island Unit 1 as the Crane
Clean Energy Center, adding roughly 835 MW of carbon-free power to the grid.
Google signed with Kairos Power for up to 500 MW of new 24/7 carbon-free nuclear
power, with the first unit targeted for 2030. Amazon has co-location and nuclear
development agreements tied to AWS data centers, including existing nuclear
capacity in Pennsylvania and small modular reactor projects in Washington and
Virginia.

For an AI lab, hyperscaler, utility, or data-center developer, the public story
still tends to fixate on chips and model releases. The siting meeting is more
physical. Which sites can actually be energized? Which megawatts are useful at
the hour and location where compute needs them? Who pays for the grid upgrades?
What happens if a model roadmap assumes capacity before the power system can
deliver it?

The decision is not simply "buy more electricity." The question is which input
actually slows deployment, how early it has to be secured, and who gains leverage
when compute demand runs into infrastructure built on a slower clock.

## Synopsis

A frontier AI campus is no longer just a chip-procurement problem: the binding constraints are electrical load, cooling, permits, and grid connection on a schedule utilities are not built for.
