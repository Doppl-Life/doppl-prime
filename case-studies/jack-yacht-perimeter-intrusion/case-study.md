# Case Study: The Swim-Up Boarding

A large private yacht had crew onboard, a night watch, access controls, and CCTV.
It still had a basic security failure.

An intruder entered the water from shore, swam out to the vessel, used a tender as
a step, climbed aboard, and reached the interior before anyone stopped him. The
tender mattered because it turned the yacht's height above the water from a barrier
into a ladder.

The embarrassment was not that the yacht had no security. It had the visible parts
of security: cameras, crew, procedures, and people watching the obvious approaches.
The missed approach was lower, slower, and quieter. It came through the water at
night, where reflections, darkness, marine life, debris, cleaning work, small craft,
and ordinary crew movement can all create noise.

The owner and guests do not want the yacht to feel like a prison. The captain does
not want a security system that screams all night because a turtle, floating bag,
or crew member appears in the wrong place. The security team needs a warning early
enough to act before someone touches the tender, hull, swim platform, or guest
spaces.

What would you recommend for detecting and responding to a swimmer or diver
approaching the yacht before they board?

## Synopsis

Despite crew, a night watch, and CCTV, an intruder swam out from shore, used the tender as a step, and reached the yacht's interior; the waterline perimeter was unguarded.
