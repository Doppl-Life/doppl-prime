# Case Study: The Missing Driver In The Traffic Stop

Traffic enforcement assumes a human driver. Someone speeds, rolls a stop, drifts
across a lane, looks down at a phone, drives impaired, forgets paperwork, ignores a
registration deadline, misses an inspection, or gives an officer a reason to pull
the car over.

Tesla-scale autonomy tests that assumption directly. Texas now requires commercial
automated-vehicle authorization for passenger or cargo service without a human
driver. California has moved in the same direction from the other side: its new AV
rules create notices of noncompliance when an autonomous vehicle commits a moving
violation, and the notice goes to the manufacturer or operator rather than to a human
driver who is not there.

That sounds like a technical enforcement update. It is bigger than that.

Traffic stops are one of the most common police-citizen contacts in the United
States. In 2022, about 49 million U.S. residents age 16 or older reported contact
with police; among police-initiated contacts, drivers in traffic stops were the
largest category. A stop can remain a simple warning or ticket. It can also become a
search, a warrant check, a vehicle tow, a forfeiture event, a license suspension, an
insurance surcharge, or a court appearance.

The revenue story is uneven. Traffic fines are not a large share of state and local
revenue in aggregate, but they matter intensely in some places. Some towns, courts,
vendors, and law practices are built around high-volume traffic enforcement. Fuel
taxes are already under pressure from EVs and efficient vehicles, and road-usage
charging programs in states such as Oregon and Utah show the replacement logic:
charge by mile instead of by gallon or by incident.

Autonomous vehicles do not make state power disappear. They change where it can
attach. A human driver can be stopped, cited, questioned, searched, licensed,
suspended, tested, and priced. A fleet vehicle can be logged, geofenced, remotely
contacted, restricted by permit, or charged by mile. California's new AV rules point
there: operators must respond to first responders, obey emergency geofences, report
more data, and can face operating restrictions.

The familiar public concern is whether robotaxis will block traffic, pick up at the
airport, or kill driving jobs. The less familiar question is what happens to a legal
and fiscal apparatus built around fallible individual drivers when the driver is no
longer the operating unit.
