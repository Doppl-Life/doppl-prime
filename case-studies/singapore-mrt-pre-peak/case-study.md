# Case Study: Singapore Rail Peak Crowding

Singapore's rail system has a morning peak problem. The trains and platforms are
most stressed in a narrow commute window, while nearby time bands have more spare
capacity. Building new rail capacity is slow and expensive, and the pressure is
not evenly distributed across every line, station, or group of riders.

Some commuters have no real flexibility. School, caregiving, shift work,
connections, and employer expectations fix their travel time. Others could move
earlier or later if the tradeoff were visible enough and the rule were simple.

The rail authority has fare gates, stored-value and account-based payment, travel
history, station-level data, and the ability to communicate time-window rules.
But any change has to be legible, automatic, adjustable, and fair enough not to
punish people whose schedules cannot move.

What could shift enough flexible trips out of the most crowded window to make the
system feel better, without building new track?

## Synopsis

Singapore's rail system is stressed in a narrow morning-peak window while adjacent time bands sit underused; some commuters can shift their travel, many cannot.
