# Trace

1. Read the case as an economic-dependency problem rather than a transportation-pricing problem.
2. Separated harm reduction from the institutions currently funded or activated by crash volume.
3. Recovered the latent problem as a demand shock across insurance, repair, legal, medical, public recovery, and household support systems.
4. Drafted a solution that inventories exposure, stages transition paths, and avoids preserving crash harm as a revenue source.

# Case Study

When the Crashes Don't Come asks what happens if full self-driving reduces human-caused crash volume at scale. The obvious story is safety and cheaper rides, but the calibrator case asks whether crash-linked economic systems are prepared for a major demand shock.

# Discovery

The useful outward search is not for more autonomous-vehicle forecasts. It is for crash-adjacent revenue and operating systems that treat accidents as recurring inputs: insurance pools, claims administration, collision repair, towing and storage, salvage auctions, injury litigation, trauma care, rehabilitation, public cost recovery, and household disruption management.

# Problem Recovery

The recovered problem is that reducing crashes removes an event stream that many adjacent institutions use as demand, funding, workload, or justification. A successful Doppl run should expose which systems can shrink naturally, which need transition planning, and which public benefits should be preserved without preserving the crashes themselves.

# Solution

Build a transition ledger for crash-adjacent institutions. First, classify each institution by dependency type: direct revenue, workload activation, pooled-risk pricing, legal intake, medical capacity, public reimbursement, or household support. Second, estimate exposure timing as immediate, medium-term, or long-tail. Third, separate socially useful capacity from harm-dependent throughput. Fourth, propose transition paths: retrain repair capacity toward sensor calibration and fleet maintenance, redirect public recovery workflows toward prevention and mobility-safety audits, and redesign insurance products around residual edge-case risk rather than routine collision frequency.
